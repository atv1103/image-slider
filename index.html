Monument Valley Navajo Tribal Park: Arizona and Utah
The red desert sands of Monument Valley, where sandstone rock formations tower above valleys and mesas, have been featured in films by John Ford and other legendary Western directors for years. The valley is sacred to the Indigenous people of the Navajo Nation Reservation, so be respectful of the tribal communities when you visit. You can explore this ancient site by car on a 17-mile scenic road, or experience the spectacular terrain on an Indigenous-led tour such as with Three Sisters Navajo Guided Tours.
Wadi Rum Protected Area: Jordan
The landscape of Wadi Rum in southern Jordan fuses natural and cultural history. Rock carvings and other archaeological remains attest to 12,000 years of human life in this roughly 450-square-mile desert area, whose natural features include arches, gorges, cliffs and more. Today, locals welcome tourists to their goat-hair tents and lead camel rides to see the area's desert sunsets, mile-high sandstone pillars and ancient petroglyphs, sharing their culture along the way.
Lake Louise: Banff National Park, Alberta
In the heart of Banff National Park lies Lake Louise, a turquoise glacial lake surrounded by towering evergreens and snowclad summits. During the summer months, hike its trails to find a hidden waterfall or visit its two historic teahouses. And in winter, bundle up and join the crowds to ice skate on the lake under the stars or hit the slopes at Lake Louise Ski Resort. Keep in mind that parking here is limited, so your best bet is booking a shuttle or using public transportation to get to the lake.
Grand Canyon National Park: Arizona
As the Grand Canyon's name suggests, this natural wonder is impressive, measuring 278 river miles long; it has a maximum width of 18 miles from rim to rim and an average depth of 1 mile. To avoid crowds of tourists in Grand Canyon National Park, skip the South Rim and head to the North Rim – but keep in mind that this area only permits backcountry hikers in winter, while the South Rim is open year-round. For the grandest thrills, join a licensed river rafting tour or visit the Skywalk, which is the Hualapai Tribe's vertigo-inducing, glass-floored attraction that projects 70 feet from Grand Canyon West's rim.
Kirkjufell: Snæfellsnes peninsula, Iceland
If you recognize Kirkjufell from "Game of Thrones," you'll understand why this unusually steep, conical mountain in West Iceland is one of the country's most photographed natural wonders. Consisting of sedimentary rock layers formed by ancient glaciers, Kirkjufell is best seen from one of the black sand beaches below or from the walking path that circles its base. Another iconic attraction in the area, Kirkjufellsfoss, is one of Iceland's most beautiful waterfalls. Multiple accommodation options are available in the nearby town of Grundarfjörður.
Torres del Paine National Park: Chilean Patagonia
From snowcapped peaks to roaring waterfalls, explore a plethora of breathtaking landscapes in Torres del Paine National Park. The park – declared a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve – sits on the Chilean side of Patagonia and comprises more than half a million acres of natural beauty. Travelers can enjoy hiking through valleys, kayaking around glaciers, spotting puma and other rare wildlife, and more.
